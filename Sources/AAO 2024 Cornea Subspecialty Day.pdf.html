Treating the Signs and Symptoms of Dry Eye

Sezen Karakus MD Introduction Dry eye is a multifactorial disease of the ocular surface, char-acterized by a loss of homeostasis of the tear film leading to various ocular symptoms. The condition is etiologically driven by tear film instability and hyperosmolarity, ocular surface inflammation and damage, and neurosensory abnormali-ties. Our understanding of the pathophysiology of dry eye has evolved significantly, highlighting the importance of a nuanced approach to its diagnosis and management. This presentation aims to provide an in-depth analysis of current and emerg-ing strategies for treating dry eye, focusing on evidence-based practices and innovative therapies that target both the signs and symptoms of this pervasive condition. Outline I. Signs and Symptoms Based on Etiology A. Tear film instability B. Ocular surface inflammation and damage C. Neural pathways and sensory abnormalities II. Conventional Treatment Modalities A. Artificial tears and lubricants, warm compresses B. Tear conservation strategies: punctal plugs and moisture chamber spectacles C. Anti-inflammatory/immunomodulatory treatment agents: corticosteroids, cyclosporine, and lifitegrast D. Varenicline intranasal spray (Tyrvaya): nicotinic acetylcholine receptor agonist E. Perfluorohexyloctane (Miebo): evaporative barrier, lipid layer enhancement III. Procedural Methods A. Blepharoexfoliation (Blephex) B. Thermal therapies (eg, LipiFlow, iLux, TearCare): meibomian gland heating and expression, improved lipid secretion C. Meibomian gland manual expression and probing D. Intense pulsed light (IPL) therapy (eg, OptiLIGHT) IV. Advanced Therapeutic Approaches A. Autologous serum eye drops, platelet-rich plasma, and mesenchymal stem cells B. Scleral lenses and prosthetic replacement of the ocular surface ecosystem (PROSE) C. Amniotic membrane (eg, ProKera): ocular surface regeneration D. Cenegermin-bkbj (Oxervate): recombinant human nerve growth factor E. Novel therapeutics for dry eye and what’s in the pipeline Selected Readings

Cancer and the Eye

Afshan A Nanji MD I. Background Conjunctival melanoma is a rare but potentially life-threatening malignancy of the ocular surface. A recent study using data from the U.S. found the age-adjusted incidence rate to be 0.38 per 1 million people.1 Conjunctival melanoma arises most com-monly from primary acquired melanosis (PAM), but it can also arise de novo or from a conjunctival nevus.2 Risk of recurrence after treatment is up to 37% at 10 years,3 and risk of metastatic disease is estimated to be approximately 26% at 10 years.4 II. Vishal Jhanji MD FRCOphth “Atypical microbial keratitis” refers to corneal infections caused by micro-organisms not commonly encountered in clini-cal practice. Conjunctival Diseases: Cicatrizing and Noncicatrizing

Meraf A Wolle MD Conjunctivitis is the inflammation of ocular conjunctiva char-acterized by redness, edema, and discharge. The prevalence of conjunctivitis varies, depending on the cause. Acute conjunctivi-tis is estimated to affect 6 million in the U.S. annually. Conjunctivitis can be divided into different subcategories, including infectious and noninfectious, cicatrizing and nonci-catrizing. The most common causes of infectious conjunctivitis are viruses and bacteria. The most common noninfectious cause of conjunctivitis is allergic disease. Cicatrizing conjunctivitis (CC) is the result of conjunctival inflammation leading to the formation of a cicatrix, or scar, of the conjunctiva. Noncicatriz-ing conjunctivitis is when the conjunctival inflammation does not result in scarring. Most forms of acute conjunctivitis fall in this category. This presentation will provide an overview of conjunctivitis, focusing on the subcategories of cicatrizing and noncicatrizing conjunctivitis, and will highlight the characteristics of the dif-ferent etiologies of conjunctivitis. It is associated with an epithelial defect, presence of stromal inflammatory cells, and progressive stromal degradation and thinning. Conjunctival, episcleral, and scleral inflammation are usually present. It leads to progressive necrosis of the corneal stroma, leading to perforation and blindness. Collagen vascular diseases are responsible for approximately half of the noninfectious cases of PUK. Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is the most common collagen vascular disorder that causes PUK, responsible for 34% of cases of noninfectious PUK. Since PUK is more common in people with collagen vascular disorders (especially RA), it is more common in females than in males. II. Etiology and Pathogenesis Peripheral thinning or melt may be due to infectious or immune-mediated etiology. First and foremost, an infectious origin of the PUK must be excluded. In any case of limbal thinning associated with corneal infiltrate, it is mandatory to assume an infectious etiology until proved otherwise. The most likely microbial organisms that can cause PUK include Staphylococcus, fungal organisms, and herpes simplex virus. The peripheral cornea is prone to thinning and melt due to proximity to limbal conjunctiva, blood vessels, and lymphatics, which extend approximately 0.5 mm into the clear cornea. Corneal Crosslinking: New Protocols

Farhad Hafezi FARVO MD PhD This presentation highlights new developments in corneal crosslinking (CXL) and how they overcome the limitations of the Dresden Protocol, which was the gold standard for over 20 years. The Dresden Protocol involves epithelium debridement to allow riboflavin to saturate the corneal stroma, followed by 30 minutes of UV-A irradiation at 3 mW/cm² to deliver a fluence of 5.4 J/cm². Although effective, the protocol is slow. It also requires a minimum stromal thickness of 400 µm to protect the corneal endothelium from UV-induced damage, but recent studies suggest moderately higher UV fluences can be safely delivered. Although higher UV intensities can deliver the required flu-ence faster, accelerated CXL initially resulted in less corneal stiffening due to oxygen diffusion being rate-limiting in the CXL process (see Figure 1); rapid UV delivery consumes oxygen faster than it can diffuse into the stroma. A consensus formed that the best trade-off between speed and stiffening efficacy was the use of a 10-minute, 9 mW/cm² intensity UV irradiation protocol. Figure 1. Accelerating the delivery of 5.4 J/cm² UV fluence during cor-neal crosslinking with higher intensity illumination for shorter periods results in diminishing strenthening effects. To address these issues, adaptations were made to CXL pro-tocols, such as fractionated UV energy delivery and 100% oxy-gen goggles, but these require expensive and bulky equipment. Recent advancements have led to an accelerated CXL protocol that delivers 10 J/cm² in 9 minutes and 15 seconds, maintaining the same efficacy as the Dresden Protocol without supplemental oxygen. Attempts to overcome the Dresden Protocol’s 400-µm thickness limit had mixed success. Hypo-osmolar riboflavin to swell the cornea resulted in unpredictable thickening, and riboflavin-soaked contact lens–assisted CXL inhibited oxy-gen diffusion and resulted in suboptimal stiffening. Modern protocols now adapt the technique to the cornea. The sub400 algorithm allows UV intensity duration to be tailored to each patient’s corneal thickness, ensuring safe crosslinking down to a 70-µm un-crosslinked safety region (see Figure 2). This protocol has successfully stopped ectasia progression in corneas as thin as 198 µm. Figure 2. The sub400 uses a pachymetry-guided irradiation duration to safely crosslink the anteiror stroma and leave approximately 70 µm of un-crosslinked cornea at the base of the stroma to protect the corneal endothelium. Epithelial debridement, a major drawback of Dresden Proto-col CXL and other “epi-off” protocols, is painful and increases infection risk until the epithelium regrows. CXL effectively sterilizes the cornea, but the healing process increases the risk of infection and requires antimicrobial prophylaxis. Epi-on CXL aims to avoid these issues by maintaining an intact epithelium. Achieving epi-on CXL involves delivering riboflavin into the stroma, either through iontophoresis or penetration enhancers, though this adds time and complexity. Epi-on protocols also need to address the epithelium’s absorption of UV energy and barrier to oxygen diffusion, often requiring supplemental oxy-gen. Deep Anterior Lamellar Keratoplasty

Rajesh Fogla MD FRCS Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty (DALK) is a corneal trans-plant surgery primarily used to treat anterior corneal patholo-gies while preserving the patient’s own healthy endothelium. This procedure is advantageous over full-thickness penetrating keratoplasty (PK) because it reduces the risk of endothelial rejection and postoperative complications associated with PK. Indications DALK is indicated for patients with corneal stromal diseases that do not affect the endothelium. These conditions include keratoconus, corneal scars from infections or trauma, and stro-mal dystrophies. By retaining the patient’s endothelium, DALK provides better long-term graft survival and decreases the need for chronic immunosuppression. Surgical Technique The DALK procedure involves the removal of the anterior cor-neal stroma up to the Descemet membrane. Several techniques can be used to separate the stroma from the pre-Descemet layer (PDL) or Descemet membrane, including air injection (big bubble technique), viscoelastic dissection, hydrodissection, or manual layer-by-layer dissection. The donor corneal tissue is then carefully placed and sutured onto the recipient’s cornea. Femtosecond laser can be used to create a shaped cut, allowing better donor apposition and lower induced astigmatism. Intra-operative OCT is useful to assess depth of cannula placement for air injection for big bubble DALK and for residual stromal bed estimation during manual DALK. Outcomes and Complications Clinical studies have demonstrated that DALK offers visual outcomes comparable to those of PK, with fewer risks. Visual acuity improvement is generally significant, and patients report high satisfaction. Common complications include intraoperative perforation of the Descemet membrane, which may necessitate conversion to PK, and postoperative issues such as double ante-rior chamber, graft–host interface haze, and neovasculariza-tion. Conclusion DALK is a highly effective corneal transplant technique for anterior corneal diseases. It offers substantial benefits over traditional PK, particularly in reducing the risk of endothelial rejection and other complications. As surgical techniques and instruments continue to improve, the success rates and visual outcomes of DALK are expected to be further enhanced, mak-ing it a preferred option for suitable patients. The Environmental Impact of Waste Reduction Strategies in Ophthalmic Surgery

Aakriti Garg Shukla MD Introduction/Background There is growing awareness that the health-care sector accounts for a substantial percentage of greenhouse gas emissions from industrialized nations and that climate change poses a major threat to global public health, particularly in the most vulner-able populations.1 This has focused increased attention on the problem of unnecessary operating room (OR) waste.2 Sustainability in Ophthalmology—An Obligation and Opportunity Ophthalmology has the highest surgical volumes of any spe-cialty, with cataract surgery being one of the most frequently performed operations in all of medicine. Approximately 29 million cases were performed globally in 2019, including 4.2 million in the United States.3 Any unnecessary surgical waste on this scale has enormous financial and environmental impacts and threatens the long-term global sustainability of this vital procedure.2,4 In large surveys of North American and European cataract surgeons’ attitudes toward OR waste, more than 90% of respondents were concerned about climate change, felt that OR waste is excessive and must be reduced, and wanted more reusable surgical products.5,6 Sustainable Care Globally Several institutions across the globe prioritize environmentally and economically sustainable care. The Aravind Eye Care Sys-tem (AECS) is known for long-standing practices that include reusing surgical instruments that are labeled for single use (such as phacoemulsification tubing), simultaneous surgery on mul-tiple patients in the same OR, not changing gowns or gloves between patients, and patients wearing street clothes (without patient gowns) in the OR. These practices are not allowed in most Western surgical facilities, largely because of the assumed risk of cross-contamination causing postoperative endophthal-mitis.2 Despite substantial differences from Western institutions, outcomes at AECS are remarkable. A retrospective registry study found the AECS postoperative endophthalmitis rate to be 0.04% in 2 million consecutive cataract surgeries that employed these standard OR protocols, including the reuse of instruments and multiuse eye drops, from 2011 to 2018.7 Dur-ing an overlapping period from 2013 to 2017, the American Academy of Ophthalmology (AAO) Intelligent Research in Sight (IRIS®) Registry reported a virtually identical 0.04% postoperative endophthalmitis rate in 8.5 million consecutive cataract surgeries.8 Perioperative Pharmaceutical Waste Perioperative pharmaceutical waste is a major source of OR waste. A study evaluating 4 U.S. surgical sites—a private ASC, a private tertiary care center, a private outpatient center, and a Veteran’s Administration hospital—found that an average of $148 in meds are discarded per cataract surgery. This is not surprising, as we know that only 1-3 drops are used from 5-15 mL bottles in our cases, and the bottles are often disposed of following surgical cases due to local regulations.9 A given surgical center wastes an estimated $195K annually in unused pharmaceutical products for cataract surgery. Given that 4.2 million cataract surgeries take place annually in the U.S., approximately $620 million is lost in wasted medications. A cost analysis evaluating multiuse dilation drops prior to cataract surgery and multiuse betadine usage prior to intravitreal injec-tions found that over a 5-year period, 34,000 bottles of dilating drops and 41,000 bottles of betadine could be saved if these medications were multidosed instead of used once and disposed of after 1 use. Multidosing practices would save institutions almost $300,000 over 5 years.10 Perioperative drug waste also contributes to drug shortages that limit the availability of vital medications. Next Steps/Advocacy Growing interest and concern from ophthalmologists about the environmental impact of the health-care center has created sev-eral opportunities to become involved. The AAO, the American Society of Cataract and Refractive Surgery, and the European Society of Cataract and Refractive Surgeons, as well as 40+ global organizations, have made a commitment to collaborate to make eye care and surgery more environmentally sustainable through an organization called EyeSustain (EyeSustain.org). Gender Equity in the Physician Workforce

Julia A Haller MD There are 18,948 active ophthalmologists in the United States, according to the most recent “2022 Physician Specialty Data Report” from the Association of American Medical Colleges. A total of 13,786 ophthalmologists (72.8%) are men, and 5,152 (27.2%) are women. In residencies, women are much closer to achieving parity. Gender disparities have been demonstrated at many levels, including in leadership roles, academic ranks and promotion, income, editorial boards, publications, “KOL” positions, invited podium presentations, and many more. Advances have been shown to occur when women are spe-cifically recruited, when they have mentors, and when senior female ophthalmologists are on program committees and in senior authorship roles. The emergence of female professional societies (Women in Ophthalmology, Women in Retina) and women’s groups within existing societies (Women in Eye and Vision Research, ARVO) has increased leadership training and mentorship. These advances offer templates for extending diver-sification and inclusion work beyond gender to other underrep-resented minorities in ophthalmology. Selected Readings
