How to Read Corneal Topography

  • By Sona N Shah, MDAdd to My Bookmarks View
  • Mark Complete
  • Remove Comments You’re starting your first-year rotation in cornea clinic and need a quick breakdown on how to read corneal topography. Here’s a simple step-by-step approach. It’s important to note that while “topography” and “tomography” are used interchangeably, each is technically a separate entity.
  • Topography maps the surface curvature of the anterior cornea via the projection of keratoscopic rings (mires) (devices such as the Orbscan and Atlas).
  • Tomography maps both the anterior and posterior surfaces of the cornea (devices such as the Pentacam and Galilei).

How Are These Maps Helpful?

  • Detecting and managing corneal conditions such as keratoconus and pellucid marginal degeneration (Does the patient have steepening? Do they need cross-linking?)
  • Screening patients for refractive surgery (Is the patient at risk for corneal ectasia?)
  • Preoperative planning for cataract surgery (Does the patient have regular astigmatism?)
  • Postoperative suture removal for patients’ status post penetrating keratoplasty (Which sutures should you remove at their post-op visits?)

Pentacam Scan Basics

Images courtesy of the Academy and Sona Shah, MD A Pentacam scan will show you four maps as shown below:

  • Axial/sagittal curvature (top left): Provides the steepness at different points in dioptric values
  • Corneal thickness (bottom left): Provides the corneal thickness at different locations
  • Elevation of the anterior surface (top right): Displays the curvature of the cornea in comparison to a best-fit sphere
  • Elevation of the posterior surface (bottom right): Displays the curvature of the posterior cornea in comparison to a best-fit sphere (Note: The Pentacam utilizes Scheimpflug technology to generate a three-dimensional representation of the cornea.)

Standardized Approach in Clinic

Your approach will vary on a case-to-case basis but having a standardized approach will always help you to efficiently evaluate scans.

  • Verify the patient and laterality.
  • Confirm the quality and centration of the scan.
  • Look at the maps: The axial map (evaluate for steepening/ flattening or astigmatism) Red / Orange = Steeper || Blue / Purple = Flatter , the thickness map (evaluate for thickening/ thinning) Red / Orange = Thinner || Blue / Purple = Thicker , the elevation maps (check for any posterior elevation/signs of forme fruste keratoconus).
  • Look at the values in the statistics boxes (left side for Pentacam): K1, K2, astig values, the Q values, the pachy apex, thinnest pachy and Kmax values.

Cornea Front — Anterior Values

  • Quality score: Will alert that the scan needs to be repeated
  • K1, K2, Km: Km = the average of K1 and K2; K1 is the flattest meridian of the cornea (in dioptric power) and K2 is the steepest meridian of the cornea (in dioptric power)
  • The Q-value: Ideal Q-value is -0.26 (prolate)
  • Negative values = prolate (steeper in the center, flatter peripherally); the more negative values can suggest keratoconus or hyperopic correction.
  • Positive values = oblate (flatter in the center, steeper peripherally); the more positives values can suggest myopic correction.
  • A typical aspheric cornea has a Q value between -0.20 and -0.45.
  • Clinical application: It’s important to consider the Q-value when selecting an intraocular lens for cataract surgery — generally a Q value within -0.20 and -0.45 is considered ideal for multifocal IOLs (in order to minimize aberrations).

Pupil Center Values

  • Kmax: Steepest point on the anterior curvature of the cornea
  • Pachy apex: The thickness of the cornea at the apex
  • Thinnest location: The thinnest corneal point

Clinical Applications

Astigmatism

Regular Astigmatism

  • Definition: Symmetric steepening along a corneal meridian
  • Topo findings: Symmetric “bow tie” with the steep and flat axes along one meridian
  • Clinical application: You typically want your patients to have regular astigmatism if you are placing a toric IOL for cataract surgery.
  • Irregular Astigmatism Definition: Nonuniform steepening of the cornea
  • Topo findings: Flat and steep axes are not along the same meridian.
  • Clinical application: Patients with irregular astigmatism are classically not great candidates for toric IOL; irregular astigmatism may raise suspicion for other corneal conditions such as dry eye syndrome, salzmaan nodules, etc.
  • With-the-Rule Astigmatism Definition: Steepening along the vertical meridian
  • Topo findings: As below
  • Against-the-Rule Astigmatism Definition: Steepening along the horizontal meridian
  • Topo findings: As below
Keratoconus
  • Definition: Corneal ectatic disorder characterized by progressive thinning and steepening
  • Topography can be utilized for screening, diagnosing and monitoring for progression.
  • Topo findings: Inferior steepening with correlating corneal thinning, posterior elevation, high irregular astigmatism The following findings raise suspicion for keratoconus:
  • Central K: > 48.7 D (forme fruste with K 47.2–48.7 D)
  • I-S value (inferior-superior asymmetry index): > 1.2 D
  • Astigmatism: > 2.5 D
  • Posterior elevation: > 18–20 microns
Pellucid Marginal Degeneration (PMD)
  • Definition: Corneal ectatic disorder characterized by peripheral and inferior thinning
  • Topo findings: In contrast to keratoconus, PMD has a “crab- claw” pattern with inferior and peripheral rather than central thinning and with protrusion of the cornea above the area of thinning.
Pterygium
  • Definition: Growth of an ocular surface tissue that can extend onto the cornea
  • Topo findings: Flattening in the horizontal meridian, with-the-rule astigmatism
Salzmaan’s Nodules
  • Definition: Deposits of extracellular matrix material on the corneal surface
  • Topo findings: Irregular astigmatism
Refractive Surgery Screening
  • Patients undergoing LASIK (laser-assisted in situ keratomileusis) and PRK (photorefractive keratectomy) require screening to ensure that they are good candidates (not at high risk for post-refractive ectasia).
  • PTA (percent tissue altered) is utilized to estimate the risk of post-operative ectasia.
  • PTA = FT + AD/CCT (where FT = flap thickness, AD = ablation depth, CCT = central corneal thickness)
  • PTA values greater than 40% are considered high risk for ectasia.
Suture Removal After Penetrating Keratoplasty (PKP)
  • Typically for adults, suture removal begins about six months status post-operatively after Remove the suture based on the steep axis of astigmatism. Removing sutures along the steep axis will lead to corneal flattening. About the author: Sona N.
